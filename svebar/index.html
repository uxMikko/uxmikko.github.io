The Problem

Microbiologists and epidemiologists were monitoring national bacterial outbreaks using a system decades past its design date — and it was making their job slower and more error-prone.

Background

A national surveillance platform where broken trust had real public health consequences

SVEBar is the national platform used by microbiologists and epidemiologists across Sweden to monitor and report multiresistant bacteria. Early outbreak detection depends on this system working well.

The existing platform was fragmented and deeply inconsistent. Monitoring configurations were buried in nested menus. Notifications weren't trusted. Users had started manually cross-checking data rather than relying on what the system told them.

This wasn't a cosmetic problem. Lack of trust in alerts means slower response to potential outbreaks.

My Role

Solo designer embedded with the Public Health Agency team for the full engagement

UX/UI Designer at LH+P, working embedded with the Folkhälsomyndigheten team. I was the only designer on the project across the full engagement — running research, leading ideation, building and testing wireframes, and handing off a complete design system aligned to public sector standards.

I had direct access to the epidemiologists and microbiologists who used the system. No researcher in between — which meant the findings went directly into design decisions.

Discovery

Observation first, then structured interviews — because watching sessions gave context that asking couldn't

I spent the early phase watching before asking. Observing actual monitoring sessions with microbiologists gave me a much clearer picture than interviews alone would have. Then I ran structured workshops and interviews with microbiologists and epidemiologists across regional labs and national infection units, mapping the full monitoring workflow from a new positive sample arriving to a potential outbreak flag being raised.

1
The interface wasn't the problem — opacity was

The biggest usability problem was that the monitoring configuration logic was invisible. Users had set up rules weeks or months earlier and couldn't recall what they'd done. The system gave no persistent feedback about what was configured or why.

2
Notifications weren't trusted — and users had workarounds

Several users had stopped relying on alerts entirely because alerts had fired incorrectly in the past. They were manually checking data daily. The system's primary value proposition wasn't functioning.

3
Navigation was deeply nested for common tasks

Routine configuration required multiple levels of navigation with no shortcuts and no visible state. Getting back to where you were required starting over.

4
Synonym matching was a daily manual labour task

Lab submissions included free-text bacteria identification. Because terminology wasn't standardised, scientists had to manually reconcile terms against a synonym database constantly. The database existed — working with it was painstakingly slow.

Baseline going in

Manual workarounds were the clearest signal. Expert users — epidemiologists with decades of domain knowledge — had stopped trusting the system's alerts. That set the redesign target clearly: restore trust in notifications, and make configuration visible and recoverable.

The Hard Part

A regulated environment, expert users who couldn't describe their needs, and a trust problem that design alone couldn't fully fix

Regulated public sector constraints

Modern and usable, but couldn't stray far from Swedish government design conventions. Every visual decision needed to be defensible within those standards.

Expert users with unstated mental models

The users knew exactly what they needed. They just couldn't always describe it in UX terms. Ethnographic observation became the research method — not surveys or questionnaires.

A backend trust gap design couldn't close alone

Users had stopped trusting alerts partly because alerts had actually misfired — a backend problem, not a design one. I had to design for the future state where the system was reliable while being honest about what design alone could achieve.

Slow-moving institutional approvals

In a national public health agency, decisions on platform direction required stakeholder sign-off at multiple levels. Moving quickly meant sequencing what could ship independently of that process.

How I Navigated It

The navigation structure didn't match the monitoring mental model — so I restructured around how the work actually happens

Once I understood the real workflow end-to-end, the interface problems became obvious. You don't think "configure a rule, then check a dashboard" — you think "I want to know if something unusual is happening with this bacteria in this region." The architecture needed to reflect that logic.

For the notification trust issue: I designed transparent alert states with explicit configuration summaries. Users could see exactly what rule had fired and why. That's a design contribution to trust restoration, even if the full fix required backend work.

For synonym management: I proposed a redesigned flow that replaced a slow database interface with a guided matching process — turning a task scientists dreaded into one they could move through quickly.

How Might We

Make monitoring configurations permanently visible — so users can trust what the system is doing without navigating back through settings to check?

Design

Dashboard-first architecture, alert transparency, and a synonym flow redesigned from scratch

With the architecture reframed around the monitoring mental model, I moved from discovery into design — building across the dashboard, configuration flows, and alert system simultaneously, keeping the system coherent at every level of access.

1
Dashboard-based architecture

Replaced the deeply nested menu system. Primary view shows monitored bacteria, current status, and active alerts in one place — matching the real monitoring mental model.

2
Step-based configuration flows

For monitoring rules, with persistent state feedback. Users can see what's configured and when it was set up — addressing the core "I don't know what I've set up" problem directly.

3
Visual alert system with rule summaries

Each alert shows which rule triggered it, with prioritisation logic. The first real alert transparency the platform had — users could finally understand why an alert had fired.

4
Synonym management redesign

Replaced a slow, difficult database interface with a guided matching flow for mapping incoming lab terms to known organisms. Turned a task scientists dreaded into a one-click confirmation workflow.

5
Design system aligned to Swedish public sector standards

Where the standards didn't cover specific patterns — alert priority logic, configuration summary states — I documented proposed extensions with rationale, keeping them alignable with existing standards.

Testing & Iterations

Walkthrough sessions structured around the three biggest pain points identified in research

Wireframe walkthroughs with lab users and regional infection unit staff. Scenarios were built around the three highest-anxiety moments: setting up a new monitoring rule, interpreting an active alert, and updating an existing configuration.

Key validation: Users found the dashboard structure immediately legible. The configuration flow — particularly the persistent state summary — addressed the "I don't know what I've set up" problem directly. One participant noted they could for the first time quickly verify their configurations without navigating back through the settings tree.

Results

Configuration visibility restored — and the daily manual workaround eliminated

Rebuilt trust in alerts through explicit rule summaries and transparent trigger logic
0 nesting to reach monitoring status — dashboard replaced multi-level navigation
↓ manual cross-checking eliminated by configuration visibility and alert transparency
1 handoff design system delivered to agency for ongoing development without a designer

What I'd do differently

Establish a quantitative baseline at the start. We knew users were doing manual workarounds, but we didn't track how often or how long. A simple baseline — even just "how many times a week do you manually check data that the system should surface automatically" — would have made this outcomes section significantly stronger.

Push harder on the alert trust issue as a product problem, not just a design problem. The backend logic gap was known early. Getting it flagged formally as a product requirement — rather than a future improvement — would have led to a more complete fix.

Design System

Public sector standards applied and extended — with documented rationale for patterns the standards didn't cover

All work built to Swedish public sector design standards. Where the standards didn't cover specific patterns — particularly around the alert system's priority logic and the configuration summary states — I documented proposed extensions with rationale, keeping them alignable with the existing standards for future development without a designer present.

The design system was a deliberate handoff artefact, not a byproduct. The agency needed to keep building after the engagement ended. Every extension included usage guidance and edge cases.

Looking ahead — the AI opportunity

The synonym matching problem I redesigned is exactly the kind of task AI autosuggestions are built for

The database was already there — what was missing was speed. A model trained on bacteriology terminology could surface likely matches as scientists type, turning a slow manual lookup into a one-click confirmation. The design pattern I built — guided matching with human validation — maps directly onto how AI-assisted workflows should be structured: the model suggests, the expert confirms.

That's the kind of AI integration that reduces friction without removing the human judgment that actually matters in public health surveillance.
