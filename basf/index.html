Merging Three Developer Platforms into One Unified Experience

Three internal tools, three overlapping user groups, no shared language — and a business decision already made to consolidate. I reframed the brief around boundary clarity and drove three Product Owners to a shared product vision.

Insight

Users weren't confused because the platforms were different — they were confused because they overlapped without clear boundaries. An IA problem, not a UI problem.

Action

Reframed the brief around boundary clarity. Built a product brief from discovery that gave all three POs a shared basis for every prioritisation call that followed.

THE PROBLEM

Three internal platforms, three overlapping user groups, no shared language — and a mandate to consolidate already decided.

BACKGROUND

A consolidation brief without a shared definition of the problem

BASF ran three internal developer tools: Argus, the Data Science Platform, and the AppStore. Each had grown organically to serve specific needs. Together, they created fragmented workflows, duplicated access requests, and a cognitive overhead nobody had officially agreed to own.

The user base spanned three very different personas: experienced developers building internal apps, data scientists running pipelines and models, and novice internal users just trying to find and use what already existed. Designing for that spread — across a consolidated platform — was the real challenge.

"Consolidation wasn't a UI problem — it was an information architecture problem."

DISCOVERY

Understanding the platforms before asking users about them

Before talking to anyone, I reviewed platform documentation, analytics, and tutorial videos. I wanted to understand how each platform understood itself before hearing how users experienced it.

1
Platform audit

Reviewed Argus, Data Science Platform, and AppStore documentation, analytics, and video walkthroughs independently before any interviews.

2
Stakeholder interviews — all three Product Owners

Mapped ownership, priorities, and where they disagreed. Three POs meant three roadmaps and three definitions of done — understanding the fault lines early was essential.

3
User interviews — developers and data scientists

Ran interviews across multiple internal teams. The consistent finding: users weren't confused by the platforms being different. They were confused by the overlap — the same app in two places, the same workflow triggering different permissions depending on where you started.

KEY FINDING

Users had stopped trusting that the right path existed — not that they couldn't find it

What was measured going in: Platform fragmentation caused developers to context-switch between tools for a single workflow. Novice users had no guided onboarding path and frequently escalated to support. These were the baselines the redesign needed to move.

THE HARD PART

Three POs, a hackathon forcing function, and technical constraints that weren't visible until mid-discovery

Stakeholder alignment

Three POs meant three roadmaps, three definitions of done, and sometimes three incompatible priorities. I built a feature prioritisation table scored on impact, effort, and technical feasibility — it became the decision tool in sprint planning. When opinions clashed, the table moved the conversation from preference to evidence.

Technical constraints

Engineering was building on Backstage, which added constraints that weren't fully visible until mid-discovery. Some things users needed weren't buildable in the current phase — I had to design for what was feasible while keeping the long-term direction honest.

Hackathon forcing function

An internal hackathon forced a prioritisation call ahead of schedule. I drafted a brief that reframed the decision — not "which feature do we want" but "which user problem is most urgent." That shift gave the POs a shared basis for the call.

Handoff gap

No formal design handoff process existed before this engagement. I moved design ticket conversations from GitHub into Figma comments — a small change that gave engineering context directly at the design level, significantly reducing back-and-forth.

HOW MIGHT WE…

Consolidate three overlapping platforms into a single unified experience that serves developers, data scientists, and novice users — without making any of them feel like an afterthought?

DESIGN

End-to-end journeys, a guided wizard, and a handoff workflow built from scratch

With the product brief agreed, I moved from discovery into design — building across all three personas simultaneously, keeping each flow legible without making the system feel like three products bolted together.

1
End-to-end user journeys

For app creation, data pipeline management, and dashboard consumption — mapped across developer, data scientist, and novice user paths.

2
Low and mid-fidelity wireframes

Three core flows: the app creation wizard, data pipeline onboarding, and the unified app dashboard. Iterated through two rounds of feasibility-driven revision.

3
App creation wizard

Reduced the onboarding path for novice users from a fragmented multi-platform process across three tools to a single guided flow.

4
BASF Atoms extensions

All components built within the BASF Atoms design system. Where patterns didn't exist — primarily the app wizard step flow and unified dashboard state management — I proposed and documented extensions, keeping them aligned with the existing token structure.

TESTING & ITERATIONS

Walkthrough sessions built around scenarios, not open feedback

I ran walkthrough sessions with POs and developers before each handoff, structured around specific scenarios rather than open feedback. Two key flows — the app wizard and the permissions model — went through two rounds of iteration based on feasibility blockers surfaced during walkthroughs.

I also mentored a master's student through an internship project delivered on the same platform, which served as an additional validation layer for the wizard flow — the student's output was integrated into the platform.

WHAT I'D ADD NOW

If I ran any sessions directly with data scientists or novice end-users (not just POs and engineers), those would go here. Even one or two walkthroughs counts. Format: "Ran [X] user walkthroughs with internal [role] users, testing against 3 scenarios: [list them]."

RESULTS

Unified product vision — three legacy systems, one agreed direction

3 POs aligned on a single product brief for the first time
1 wizard replaced a fragmented multi-tool onboarding path for novice users
↓ friction in design–engineering handoff by moving context into Figma
1 intern mentored — output integrated directly into the live platform

WHAT I'D DO DIFFERENTLY

Push harder for direct user access earlier. A lot of discovery was mediated through Product Owners — efficient but filtered. More direct exposure would have made the prioritisation table stronger and surfaced the novice onboarding gap sooner.

Establish a quantitative baseline before starting. We knew things were fragmented, but we didn't have a number attached to it. Having one would have made this outcomes section significantly cleaner.

FINAL THOUGHTS

What I learned about navigating complexity without a clear brief

The biggest shift I made on this project was recognising that the design problem wasn't what I was originally asked to solve. The brief was about UI consolidation. The real problem was that nobody had defined the boundaries between the three systems — so they overlapped, contradicted each other, and frustrated users regardless of how good the individual interfaces were.

Reframing from UI to IA early — and getting three POs to accept that reframing — is what made the rest of the project workable. The product brief wasn't a deliverable. It was the tool that made every other decision possible.

Next step: quantitative baseline data from the wizard flow — steps-to-completion, error rates, support ticket volume before and after. That's what would turn this from a strong story into an undeniable one.
