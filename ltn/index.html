The Problem

A new law created a hard deadline. The Public Health Agency had no digital process for product registration. And the people who'd have to run it were worried automation would replace their jobs.

Background

A government agency, a legal deadline, and a registration process that had only ever existed on paper

In 2023, Sweden enacted legislation requiring all tobacco-free nicotine products — pouches, sprays, gums — to be registered with the Public Health Agency before they could be marketed. Before this law, no registration process existed. Companies were submitting product data via manual forms and email.

The agency had a grace period before the law took full effect. We had to design and ship a compliant, secure registration portal within that window. Not a beta, not an MVP with rough edges — a system that would handle legally sensitive product data under strict government standards from day one.

Complicating factor: the operations staff who'd manage submissions were worried about automation. A portal that replaced their manual review process felt threatening — regardless of how good the design was.

My Role

The only designer on a small cross-functional team — running research, design, and stakeholder alignment simultaneously

UX/UI Designer at LH+P, embedded with the Folkhälsomyndigheten team. I was the only designer across the full project — responsible for research, the full design process from wireframes through to handoff, and navigating the internal stakeholder dynamics that sat underneath the surface design problems.

The team was small: me, a few developers, a product lead, QA, and operations representatives. Being the only designer in a room full of technical and operational stakeholders meant every design decision needed a rationale that worked for multiple audiences — not just the end user, but the agency staff who'd use it daily and the legal team validating compliance.

Discovery

Shadowing operations first — understanding manual workflows before proposing automated replacements

I collaborated closely with operations staff to map the existing manual process — how registrations were received, processed, and approved via email and paper forms. Understanding their workflow before proposing a replacement was critical. Jumping straight to "here's the new digital process" without that grounding would have amplified the resistance, not reduced it.

1
Manual process was error-prone and unscalable

Email-and-form submissions meant no consistent data structure, frequent incomplete submissions, and no status visibility for either the company or the agency. Processing time varied significantly by submission quality.

2
Operations valued control, not just efficiency

The operations team's hesitation wasn't irrational. They'd built expertise around manual review. Their concern was legitimate: would a portal make their judgment irrelevant? That informed a core design constraint — the system needed to enhance their oversight, not bypass it.

3
Legal precision was non-negotiable

Every label, form field, and error message had to use officially approved language in both Swedish and English. Design decisions that would be trivial in a consumer context required legal review and formal sign-off.

4
Sign-off was a bottleneck that threatened the whole model

Under the manual model, operations had to personally approve every registration. At scale — with hundreds of submissions anticipated — that would collapse. Solving this without removing their involvement was the central design challenge.

Baseline going in

Every registration arrived via email. No structured data, no validation, no status visibility, no duplicate detection. Operations were manually reviewing everything before any approval. The baseline was clear — and so was the design direction: structure, automate where safe, preserve human judgment where it matters.

The Hard Part

A hard legal deadline, resistant stakeholders, legally controlled content, and a security standard that limited design flexibility

A hard deadline with zero flexibility

The law had already passed. The portal had to launch before the grace period expired. That meant no extended research phase, no phased rollout — a complete, compliant product had to ship on time.

Stakeholder resistance to automation

Operations staff had legitimate concerns about what automation meant for their roles. Designing around their resistance required more than good UX — it required changing what the portal promised them.

Legally controlled language

Every piece of content — button labels, error messages, form field names — required formal sign-off. Design had to move through legal review cycles without losing momentum.

Government security requirements

Strict data security requirements constrained certain design patterns. Choices that would be simple in a consumer context required security review — adding time and limiting flexibility on interaction design.

How I Navigated It

The sign-off bottleneck wasn't a workflow problem — it was a trust problem. The solution was to make automation opt-out, not opt-in.

The central tension: operations needed to feel in control, but the model couldn't scale if every submission required personal approval. The answer was an asynchronous sign-off protocol — submissions go through automated validation and proceed unless operations flags them. They're still in the loop; they're just not a required gate for every clean submission.

This reframe was key. Instead of "automation will handle it," the message became: "you review what matters, the system handles what's clear." That distinction made the difference between resistance and adoption.

For the legal content problem, I worked in close parallel with the legal team — building design and copy review cycles into the sprint structure rather than treating them as sequential. That kept both moving without either blocking the other.

For the duplicate product issue — companies often register slight variants of the same product — I designed a "duplicate and edit" registration flow, which eliminated the biggest time cost for frequent submitters and significantly reduced the volume of error-prone manual re-entry.

How Might We

Give operations staff oversight without making them a bottleneck — so the system can scale to hundreds of submissions without losing the human judgment that makes it trustworthy?

Design

Step-by-step registration wizard, asynchronous sign-off, duplicate flow, and a dual-view for both companies and agency staff

With the asynchronous sign-off model unlocked and the duplicate flow scoped, I built across four areas simultaneously — the company-facing registration flow, the agency-facing review dashboard, the status system visible to both, and the compliance content framework that ran through everything.

1
Step-by-step registration wizard

Broke the legally complex registration process into clear, sequential steps with real-time validation. Companies know exactly what's required at each stage and get immediate feedback on incomplete or incorrect submissions — before they hit submit.

2
Duplicate product registration flow

Companies registering product variants can duplicate an existing registration and edit only what's different. Eliminated the biggest source of manual re-entry and reduced error rates for frequent submitters significantly.

3
Asynchronous sign-off protocol

Submissions proceed automatically unless flagged. Operations staff see everything, are notified of exceptions, and retain the ability to intervene at any point — but clean submissions don't wait in a queue. This was the design decision that converted internal resistance to adoption.

4
Agency staff review dashboard

Dual-interface design — what companies see and what operations sees are different views of the same submission. Ops get structured data, flag controls, and a full submission history. Status is visible to both sides throughout.

5
Compliant bilingual UI

Full Swedish and English support throughout, with all copy approved through the agency's legal and translation review. Accessible to WCAG standards. Official visual tone consistent with government product requirements.

Testing & Iterations

Internal walkthrough sessions with both user groups — company submitters and agency staff — tested separately

Low-fidelity wireframes tested in feedback sessions with both the operations team and the development team, run separately. Testing two different user groups simultaneously meant scenarios had to be designed for very different mental models — "how do I register my product" versus "how do I process and approve submissions."

Key finding from operations sessions: the asynchronous sign-off model needed clearer language explaining what "automatic approval" actually meant in practice. Participants wanted to know exactly when they'd be notified and what level of review they were still doing. That informed a dedicated onboarding step for agency staff on first use.

The duplicate registration flow tested extremely well — operations staff noted it would significantly reduce the back-and-forth currently required for variant products, validating it as the right prioritisation call.

Results

Launched on the legal deadline — and hundreds of products registered without the manual-process collapse that would have followed

What I'd do differently

Run a lightweight operations co-design session earlier. I addressed their concerns through research and by adjusting the design — but bringing operations into the design process as contributors, not just subjects, would have accelerated the trust-building that ultimately made adoption possible. The asynchronous sign-off model was designed for them; ideally it would have been designed with them.

Establish measurable baselines for the manual process before starting. We knew the email-and-form process was slow and error-prone — but we didn't quantify it. "Processing time dropped by X%" would have been a far more compelling outcome story than "workload reduced." The data was there to collect; I didn't capture it.

Design System

Government visual standards applied throughout — with a dual-context system covering both the public-facing portal and the internal agency dashboard

All design built to government visual standards — official colours, typography, and accessibility requirements. The system had to work across two distinct contexts: the public-facing registration portal (company users, potentially any browser, variable technical literacy) and the internal agency dashboard (operations staff, controlled environment, expert users).

Designing for both in the same system required clear shared foundations — component structure, spacing, colour — while allowing the two contexts to diverge in interaction density and visual complexity where appropriate. The internal dashboard could carry more information density than the public portal.
